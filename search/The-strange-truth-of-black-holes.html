The strage truth of black holes

Black holes are a mysterious figure in the universe, as we know close to nothing about them. That's why there is such a cloud around black holes, no one fully knows what they are and how they work. They break so many laws of physics, that it's just ironic.

We are going to teach you everything we know about black holes. So grab your popcorn, and relax as we explore the craziness of black holes.

How do black holes work

You might have heard about a black hole and that it is a sucking machine. And that is true, but only few know about its full capacities and how they really work.

You know gravity, (well, know one knows, is a topic of discussion, but you know enough,) and most people would know that the heavier an object is, the more gravitational pull it has. If you squeeze something, it has the same weight but uses less space. Now what if you squeeze something so much that it breaks reality itself. Well, you get a Black hole. So yes, everything can turn into a black hole as long as it is squeezed enough, but squeezing something that hard is near impossible, and we have only seen it is the death of stars.

Light with black holes

So black holes have a lot of gravitational pull because they are so dense. But before, I said that it will break reality itself. This happens because, according to our prior math, nothing was supposed to go faster than the speed of light, but black holes break that assumption. Inside the event horizon, the gravitational pull of the black hole exceeds the speed of light, meaning even light cannot escape a black hole.

But black holes don’t only trap light, but they also manipulate it. If you are looking at a light source and you put a black book between your eyes and the light, you will see the light normally apart from the section covered by the book. But if you put a black hole between a light source and your eyes (or a camera/telescope,) you will see everything in a distorted manner, even the part where the black hole is not covering the light source. This happens because when the black hole's gravity pulls on the light, before entering the black hole, it revolves around the black hole before getting sucked in.

Inside a black hole

Option 1: fall before the event horizon

Falling into a black hole this way is actually way more unusual than you might think. When falling in a black hole, you will be moving very fast, because of the black hole's gravitational gravitational pull, but you won't be feeling this. You will be moving, but quite slowly, while everything else you can see is as well as a statue. This is because our time will be different from your time. One of our seconds will be about a full day to you, and we know this thanks to Albert Einstein's spacetime theory. While you are falling down the black hole, you would die from starvation, lack of oxygen, or boredom.

Outside the black hole, we would see you moving your hands, feet and legs about a million times a second. Remember, your time is faster than our time. We would see you die very quickly, and also see your skin and bones turn into dust very quickly too (if we can actually track you while you are going inside the black hole.)

Option 2: fall close to the event horizon

If you fall close to the event horizon, the time issue will remain, and actually be magnified because we would be closer to the black hole. But the difference this time won't be about what you feel, or touch or hear or die, but how you would see.

First, we need to know how light works. Light bounces off an object, that object absorbs some light frequencies and the rest reflects back to your eyes. Black holes are Black because the light cannot go to your eye. So when you fall in, whenever you reach the event horizon (the place where light can't go to your eye) you can see nothing and nothing can see you. For you, the whole world has gone dark, and for everyone else, you disappear.

The strange stuff

When an item goes inside a black hole, the real math breaking science happens. The item will get so much force pushed down on it that it will become the shape of spaghetti and then disappear. That is where it went haywire. I said destroy items, and according to quantum physics, nothing can get fully destroyed. It could break into small parts, or turn into energy, gasses or dust (it's hard, I know) but never get fully destroyed - but here, we find that black holes can fully destroy any item. This is making us rethink everything we know about the universe.

But then, how do Black holes die? They have to be able to, just like stars or planets, but black holes are already dead stars, and killing something that's already dead is hard - but possible. The hard part is that we can't actually kill it, just prevent it from expanding. The black hole kills itself using Hawking radiation. Long things short, black holes slowly release radiation or energy, slowly killing itself. This process takes millions of years, but it's possible.

Black holes are the coolest, strangest and deadliest substance in our universe, and it's not going anywhere soon - so you better get comfortable with them. Bye.
